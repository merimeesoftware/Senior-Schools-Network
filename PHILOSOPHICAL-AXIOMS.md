> "Poetic intuition is the capturing of one's own subjectivity in a glance which descends into the unconscious and the preconscious, and emerges into consciousness by grasping things in a unifying intuition together with the self."

## V. POETIC FOUNDATIONS FOR SCIENCE & WISDOM

### The Right Ordering: Wonder Before Analysis

**From README:**
> "Poetic knowledge serves as the fertile soil for scientific inquiry, nurturing imagination and wonder as prerequisites for disciplined reasoning. Modern education often misorders this, prioritizing analysis over wonder."

**From Aquinas (_Summa Theologica_ I, Q84, A1):**
> "The soul knows bodies through the intellect."

> "Our intellect derives intellectual knowledge from sensible things... The senses are necessary to the intellect, because they supply it with phantasms."

**From Hugh of St. Victor:**
> "The third power of the soul appropriates the prior nutritional and sense-perceiving powers, using them, so to speak, as its domestics and servants. It is rooted entirely in the reason." — _Didascalicon_ I.3

> "By things already known to it, it should seek after things not known, and it requires to know of each thing not only whether it exists, but of what nature it is, and of what properties, and even for what purpose."

Poetic knowledge is learning a language by speaking it, not by picking apart every word."

## IX. TONE MANDATES

### 1. Unapologetically Classical

**Use Latin phrases without apology:**
- _Hortus conclusus_, _puer_, _pyros_, _interea_, _quid legendum_, _ars docendi_
- _Rerum Novarum_, _Summa Theologica_, _Didascalicon_

**Invoke liturgical and literary allusions:**
- Reference saints, encyclicals, psalms, and Scripture naturally
- Quote Tolkien, Chesterton, Aquinas as authorities
- Deploy medieval imagery (enclosed gardens, stargazing monks, troubadours)

**Example Tone:**
> "In this _interea_—this 'meanwhile' between crisis and catastrophe—we walk circumspectly as Saint Paul commands, redeeming the time. Our schools are not theme parks of nostalgia but cells of good living, where _pueri_ burn with gem-like flames and mothers weave stories like the Baronne de Bethune mending socks: practical, faithful, relentless."

### 2. Urgent but Not Desperate

**Convey the Crisis:**
- Cultural decay, gymnasium gap, mechanized softness
- "The end of the world was long ago" (Chesterton)
- "Books be all their eating, And ink be on their hands" (Chesterton's warning)

**Offer the Remedy:**
- Poetic knowledge as restoration, not revolution
- Hope anchored in divine order, not human schemes

**Example Tone:**
> "The gymnasium gap is not a market opportunity—it's a civilizational wound. Strong nursery homes, stalwart high schools, yet ages 7-13 wither in sedentary safety. We do not offer a curriculum; we plant an enclosed garden where adventure and liturgy conspire to form boys into men who know the weight of a sword and the cadence of a psalm."

### 3. Poetic Precision

**Every word chosen for rhythm, imagery, truth:**
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon monosyllables for punch ("boys," "fire," "stone")
- Use Latinate polysyllables for gravity ("restoration," "innocence," "connaturality")
- Echo Scripture and poetry naturally

**Rhythm Example (Good):**
> "We make still by the law in which we're made." — Tolkien (iambic meter)

**Rhythm Example (Bad):**
> "We create things according to the rules that govern our existence." (clunky, abstract)

**Imagery Example (Good):**
> "The soul, having poured itself out through the senses upon all visible things, is dissipated in countless actions. But finally, freed from the body, returns to the pureness of its simplicity." — Hugh of St. Victor

**Imagery Example (Bad):**
> "The soul interacts with physical reality and then eventually returns to a spiritual state." (lifeless)
